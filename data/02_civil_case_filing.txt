DOCUMENT: Civil Case Filing Process in India
SOURCE: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC) – Filing and Procedure

=== HOW TO FILE A CIVIL CASE IN INDIA ===

A civil case is filed under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC). The person who files the case is called the PLAINTIFF; the person against whom it is filed is the DEFENDANT.

--- STEP 1: IDENTIFY THE CAUSE OF ACTION ---
A cause of action is the set of facts that entitles the plaintiff to seek relief from the court.
It must have arisen within the period of limitation (Limitation Act, 1963).
Common civil causes of action: breach of contract, property disputes, recovery of money, matrimonial disputes.

--- STEP 2: DETERMINE THE APPROPRIATE COURT ---
- Pecuniary jurisdiction: file in the court whose monetary limit covers the value of the claim.
- Territorial jurisdiction (Order 7, CPC): file in the court where:
  - the defendant resides, carries on business, or personally works for gain; OR
  - the cause of action (wholly or partly) arose.
- Subject-matter jurisdiction: ensure the court is empowered to hear that type of case.

--- STEP 3: PREPARE THE PLAINT ---
A Plaint is the written statement of the plaintiff's claim (Order 7, CPC). It must contain:
1. Name of the court.
2. Name, description, and address of the plaintiff.
3. Name, description, and address of the defendant.
4. Facts constituting the cause of action and when it arose.
5. Facts showing the court has jurisdiction.
6. Relief(s) claimed by the plaintiff (e.g., money, injunction, declaration).
7. Statement of value of the suit for purposes of court fees and jurisdiction.
8. Signature and verification by the plaintiff.

--- STEP 4: PAY COURT FEES ---
Court fees are paid under the Court Fees Act, 1870.
Fees are calculated as a percentage of the suit value (ad valorem) or as fixed fees for specific reliefs.
Insufficient court fees will result in the plaint being returned for correction.

--- STEP 5: FILE THE PLAINT (Institution of Suit) ---
The plaint is presented to the filing counter of the appropriate court.
Along with the plaint, the plaintiff must file:
  - Vakalatnama (if represented by an advocate).
  - Copies of all documents relied upon (listed in a document schedule).
  - Copies of the plaint for service on each defendant.
Court staff number the plaint and assign a Case Number (Civil Suit No.).

--- STEP 6: SCRUTINY AND ADMISSION ---
The court examines the plaint under Order 7, Rule 9–13 CPC:
- If the plaint is in order → the suit is admitted and a case number is assigned.
- If defective → the plaint is returned with instructions to correct (Order 7, Rule 10).
- If frivolous or discloses no cause of action → rejected (Order 7, Rule 11).

--- STEP 7: ISSUANCE OF SUMMONS TO DEFENDANT ---
Once admitted, the court issues Summons to the defendant (Order 5, CPC).
The summons requires the defendant to appear before the court on a specified date.
Methods of service:
  a. Personal service (court bailiff or process server delivers directly).
  b. Registered post with acknowledgement due (RPAD).
  c. Substituted service (affixing at defendant's last known address) if personal service fails.
  d. Publication in newspaper (in exceptional cases).

--- STEP 8: DEFENDANT'S WRITTEN STATEMENT ---
After receiving summons, the defendant files a Written Statement within 30 days (extendable to 90 days maximum) – Order 8, CPC.
The written statement denies or admits each allegation in the plaint.
The defendant may also raise preliminary objections (e.g., lack of jurisdiction, limitation).
The defendant may file a Counter-claim against the plaintiff in the same written statement.

--- STEP 9: FRAMING OF ISSUES ---
The court frames Issues of Fact and Issues of Law based on the pleadings (Order 14, CPC).
Issues are the specific questions the court needs to decide.
Example issue: "Whether the plaintiff is entitled to recover ₹5 lakhs from the defendant for breach of contract dated 01-01-2020?"

--- STEP 10: DISCOVERY AND INSPECTION ---
Parties may seek discovery of documents from the other side (Order 11, CPC).
The court may order inspection of documents.

--- STEP 11: EVIDENCE STAGE ---
Plaintiff's Evidence: Plaintiff leads evidence first by filing affidavits of witnesses.
Examination-in-chief is by affidavit; witnesses are cross-examined by the opposite party.
After plaintiff's evidence, defendant leads their evidence similarly.

--- STEP 12: ARGUMENTS ---
After evidence is complete, both parties present oral arguments.
Written arguments / brief notes may also be submitted.

--- STEP 13: JUDGMENT AND DECREE ---
The court pronounces its Judgment (reasoning) and passes a Decree (operative order).
The decree specifies the relief granted or denied.
A certified copy of the decree can be obtained from the court.

--- STEP 14: EXECUTION OF DECREE ---
If the losing party does not comply voluntarily, the winning party can file an Execution Petition (Order 21, CPC) to enforce the decree.
Methods include attachment of property, arrest of judgment debtor, or garnishee orders.

--- APPEAL ---
A decree by a subordinate court can be appealed to a higher court (First Appeal).
A second appeal lies to the High Court on a substantial question of law.
Time limit for appeal: 30 days from the date of decree for District Court appeals; 90 days for High Court appeals (Limitation Act).

SOURCE: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC); Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC); Indian Evidence Act, 1872

=== DIFFERENT STAGES OF A COURT CASE ===

A court case passes through the following broad stages:

CIVIL CASE STAGES:
Stage 1: Plaint Filing (Institution of Suit)
Stage 2: Scrutiny and Admission
Stage 3: Service of Summons on Defendant
Stage 4: Appearance of Defendant; Filing of Written Statement
Stage 5: Filing of Replication (if any) by Plaintiff
Stage 6: Framing of Issues
Stage 7: Evidence – Plaintiff's evidence (PW1, PW2…)
Stage 8: Evidence – Defendant's evidence (DW1, DW2…)
Stage 9: Arguments
Stage 10: Judgment and Decree
Stage 11: Execution (if needed)
Stage 12: Appeal (optional)

CRIMINAL CASE STAGES:
Stage 1: FIR / Complaint
Stage 2: Police Investigation
Stage 3: Arrest and Remand
Stage 4: Bail Hearing
Stage 5: Chargesheet / Final Report filed in Court
Stage 6: Cognizance by Magistrate
Stage 7: Committal to Sessions (for Sessions trial)
Stage 8: Charge Framing; Accused's Plea
Stage 9: Prosecution Evidence
Stage 10: Section 313 Examination
Stage 11: Defence Evidence
Stage 12: Arguments
Stage 13: Judgment (Acquittal or Conviction)
Stage 14: Sentencing
Stage 15: Appeal (optional)

=== BAIL IN DETAIL ===

Bail is the temporary release of an accused pending investigation or trial, on executing a bail bond with or without surety.

Application for Bail – Who to approach:
- Police station (for bailable offences during investigation).
- Magistrate Court – for offences triable by Magistrate.
- Sessions Court – for offences exclusively triable by Sessions Court or when Magistrate has refused bail.
- High Court – if Sessions Court has refused bail (Section 439, CrPC).
- Anticipatory Bail: Sessions Court or High Court, before arrest.

Documents for Bail Application:
1. Bail application/petition.
2. Surety declaration with ID proof and property documents.
3. Copy of FIR.
4. Brief facts of the case.

Factors court considers for bail:
- Gravity and nature of the offence.
- Criminal antecedents of the accused.
- Prima facie case against the accused.
- Risk of the accused fleeing.
- Risk of tampering with witnesses or evidence.
- Whether the accused has roots in the community (family, property, employment).

Cancellation of Bail (Section 437(5) / 439(2) CrPC):
Bail can be cancelled if the accused:
- Violates bail conditions.
- Tampers with evidence or witnesses.
- Does not appear in court.
- Is involved in a fresh offence.

=== APPEAL PROCEDURE ===

An appeal is a challenge to a lower court's decision before a higher court.

Types of Appeals (Criminal):
1. Appeal against Acquittal: Filed by the State (Public Prosecutor) before Sessions Court or High Court. Private complainant can also appeal with court's leave.
2. Appeal against Conviction: Filed by the accused before Sessions Court (from Magistrate), High Court (from Sessions Court), or Supreme Court.
3. Appeal against Excessive / Inadequate Sentence: Both State and accused can appeal against sentence.

Types of Appeals (Civil):
1. First Appeal: On questions of both fact and law; before the next higher court.
2. Second Appeal (Section 100 CPC): Before the High Court; only on a substantial question of law.
3. Letters Patent Appeal: Before a Full Bench of the High Court against a single judge's judgment (applicable in some High Courts).
4. Special Leave Petition (SLP – Article 136, Constitution): Before the Supreme Court against any court or tribunal's judgment.

Time Limits for Appeals (Limitation Act, 1963):
- 30 days: Appeal from lower court to Sessions Court / District Court.
- 90 days: Appeal to High Court.
- 90 days: Appeal to Supreme Court (SLP).
- Condonation of delay: Court may excuse delay if sufficient cause shown.

=== ALTERNATIVE DISPUTE RESOLUTION (ADR) ===

ADR methods settle disputes without full litigation:

1. MEDIATION: Neutral mediator helps parties reach mutually agreeable settlement. Court-referred or private. Not binding unless agreement is recorded.

2. ARBITRATION: Parties refer dispute to a private arbitrator whose decision (award) is binding. Governed by the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996.

3. LOK ADALAT (Section 19–22, Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987):
   - People's Court for amicable settlement.
   - No court fees (refunded if matter is settled from court referral).
   - Award of Lok Adalat is a decree of a civil court and binding. No appeal lies.
   - Pre-litigation Lok Adalat available for cases not yet filed.

4. CONCILIATION: Similar to mediation; conciliator may propose terms. Governed by Part III of Arbitration and Conciliation Act.

5. NEGOTIATION: Direct discussion between parties; no third party involved.

=== LIMITATION PERIOD ===

The Limitation Act, 1963 sets time limits within which legal action must be brought.
Common examples:
- Suit for recovery of money: 3 years from date of default.
- Suit for recovery of immovable property: 12 years (6 years if against government).
- Complaint under Section 138, Negotiable Instruments Act (cheque bounce): 30 days from date of receiving notice after cheque dishonour.
- Consumer complaint: 2 years from date of cause of action.
After the limitation period, the right to sue is extinguished (time-barred).

=== EXECUTION OF DECREE (ORDER 21, CPC) ===

If the losing party does not comply with the decree, the decree-holder files an Execution Petition.
Modes of execution:
1. Attachment and sale of property (movable and immovable).
2. Arrest and detention of judgment debtor in civil prison (for money decrees).
3. Delivery of possession (for property disputes).
4. Attachment of salary or bank account (garnishee order).
5. Injunction enforcement (contempt of court if violated).

Court for execution: The court that passed the decree, or the court in whose jurisdiction the property / judgment debtor is located.

=== CONTEMPT OF COURT ===

Contempt of Court Act, 1971:
Civil Contempt: Wilful disobedience of court order / judgment / decree.
Criminal Contempt: Scandalising the court, prejudicing ongoing proceedings, obstructing court administration.

Punishment: Up to 6 months imprisonment OR fine up to ₹2000, or both.
High Courts and Supreme Court have inherent contempt jurisdiction.

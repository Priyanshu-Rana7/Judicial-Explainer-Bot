DOCUMENT: Criminal Case Process in India
SOURCE: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC) – Criminal Justice Workflow

=== CRIMINAL CASE PROCESS IN INDIA ===

Criminal cases in India are governed by the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC).
The government (State) prosecutes the accused through the Public Prosecutor.
The burden of proof is on the prosecution: "Beyond Reasonable Doubt."

=== STAGE 1: FIR (FIRST INFORMATION REPORT) ===

An FIR is registered when a cognizable offence is reported to the police (Section 154, CrPC).
A cognizable offence is one where police can arrest without a warrant (e.g., murder, robbery, rape).
For non-cognizable offences (e.g., assault, cheating below threshold), a complaint is filed before a Magistrate.

How to file an FIR:
1. Go to the police station having jurisdiction over the place of offence.
2. Give information orally or in writing.
3. The officer-in-charge reduces it to writing and reads it back.
4. The informant signs / puts thumb impression.
5. A copy of the FIR is given free of cost to the informant.
Zero FIR: An FIR can be registered at any police station regardless of jurisdiction and transferred later.

=== STAGE 2: POLICE INVESTIGATION ===

After FIR, the police investigate under Chapter XII of CrPC.
Steps:
- Visit the scene of crime (panchnama / inquest report).
- Recording of witness statements (Section 161, CrPC).
- Arrest of accused (with or without warrant, depending on cognizability).
- Seizure of evidence and preparation of seizure memo.
- Medical examination (Section 164A for victims of sexual assault).
- Interrogation of accused (no coercive methods allowed).
- Remand: Police may seek judicial custody (remand) from a Magistrate for further interrogation.
  - Police custody: maximum 15 days.
  - Judicial custody (jail): can continue till chargesheet / trial.

=== STAGE 3: CHARGESHEET (POLICE REPORT) ===

On completion of investigation, the police file a Chargesheet (Section 173, CrPC) before the Magistrate.
The chargesheet contains: FIR, witness statements, expert reports, and a list of accused and charges.
If the police find insufficient evidence → they file a 'Closure Report' / 'Final Report'.
The complainant can file a 'Protest Petition' against a Closure Report.

=== STAGE 4: COGNIZANCE BY COURT ===

The Magistrate takes cognizance of the offence (Section 190, CrPC) — this means the court formally acknowledges the case.
Sources of cognizance:
  a. Police report (chargesheet).
  b. Complaint by a private person.
  c. Magistrate's own knowledge.

=== STAGE 5: ISSUANCE OF PROCESS ===

After cognizance, the court issues:
- Summons: for summons-cases (less serious, punishable up to 2 years).
  The accused is called to appear before the court.
- Warrant: for warrant-cases (serious offences, punishable with more than 2 years).
  A bailable or non-bailable warrant is issued for arrest.

=== STAGE 6: BAIL ===

Bail is the temporary release of an accused pending trial.
Types:
- Bailable offence (Schedule I, CrPC): Bail is a right; police or court must grant it.
- Non-bailable offence: Bail is at the court's discretion (Section 437 CrPC for Sessions Court; Section 439 for High Court / Sessions Court).
- Anticipatory Bail (Section 438 CrPC): Applied before arrest, granted by Sessions Court or High Court.
- Default Bail (Section 167(2) CrPC): Accused is entitled to bail if chargesheet is not filed within:
  - 60 days for offences punishable with death, life imprisonment, or ≥10 years.
  - 90 days for other offences.
Bail conditions may include surety, restricting travel, and periodic reporting.

=== STAGE 7: FRAMING OF CHARGES ===

After hearing the accused and the prosecution, the court frames Charges.
If the court finds no prima facie case → it discharges the accused (Section 227 CrPC).
If charges are framed → the accused is asked to plead guilty or not guilty.
If the accused pleads guilty → the court may convict directly.
If the accused pleads not guilty → the case proceeds to trial.

=== STAGE 8: TRIAL ===

The trial procedure depends on the type of case:
A. SESSIONS TRIAL (most serious offences):
   1. Public Prosecutor opens the case.
   2. Prosecution evidence: witnesses are examined, cross-examined, re-examined.
   3. Statement of accused (Section 313 CrPC): accused is examined by the court; no sworn oath.
   4. Defence evidence: accused can lead witnesses.
   5. Arguments by both sides.
   6. Judgment by the court.

B. WARRANT CASE (moderately serious):
   - Similar to Sessions Trial; heard in Magistrate's Court.

C. SUMMONS CASE (less serious):
   - Simplified procedure.
   - Magistrate reads charges; accused pleads.
   - Evidence and judgment follow.

=== STAGE 9: JUDGMENT ===

After arguments, the court pronounces its Judgment.
If ACQUITTED: the accused is released; cannot be tried again for the same offence (double jeopardy — Article 20(2), Constitution).
If CONVICTED: the court hears arguments on sentencing and passes a Sentence.
Sentences: imprisonment (rigorous or simple), fine, probation, or both.

=== STAGE 10: APPEAL ===

Against acquittal: State / complainant can appeal to a higher court.
Against conviction: Accused can appeal.
Appeal hierarchy:
  - Magistrate → Sessions Court (first appeal).
  - Sessions Court → High Court.
  - High Court → Supreme Court (special leave petition under Article 136).
Time limit: 30 days from judgment (extendable with sufficient cause).

=== OTHER IMPORTANT PROVISIONS ===

Section 482 CrPC – Inherent powers of High Court to quash FIRs and criminal proceedings.
Section 320 CrPC – Compounding of offences (settlement between parties for minor offences).
Section 125 CrPC – Maintenance: a Magistrate can order maintenance for wife, children, and parents.
Witness Protection: Courts can order identity protection for sensitive witnesses.
Victim Statement: Courts record victim impact statements before sentencing.
